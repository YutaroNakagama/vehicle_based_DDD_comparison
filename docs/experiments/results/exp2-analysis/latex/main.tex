\begin{abstract}
Vehicle-dynamics-based drowsy driving detection (DDD) is a non-intrusive and practical approach to driver monitoring, but its performance may be degraded by two issues: class imbalance, because drowsy samples are rare, and domain shift, because driving behavior varies across drivers.
In vehicle-dynamics-based DDD, however, it remains unclear whether these issues materially affect detection performance and which countermeasures are effective.
To address this question, we construct a DDD framework using a public 87-driver simulator dataset and conduct a four-factor factorial experiment varying rebalancing strategy ($R$), training mode ($M$), distance metric ($D$), and domain membership ($G$)
Sobol--Hoeffding variance decomposition shows that training mode and rebalancing account for more than 95\% of systematic variance, with a substantial $R \times M$ interaction that reverses the optimal rebalancing strategy across training modes.
In contrast, distance metric and domain membership have no measurable effect on performance.
A subject-wise variant of SMOTE (SW-SMOTE) at ratio $r = 0.1$ with within-domain training maximizes threshold-independent discrimination (AUROC = 0.903, AUPRC = 0.648; within-domain F2 = 0.558).
These results establish a clear design priority for vehicle-dynamics-based DDD: class rebalancing and training mode selection are the dominant performance levers, whereas domain grouping configuration can be safely omitted from the optimization process.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\IEEEPARstart{D}{rowsy} driving is a leading cause of traffic accidents worldwide, accounting for an estimated 15--20\% of road fatalities~\cite{who2018}. Vehicle-dynamics-based drowsy driving detection (DDD) offers a non-intrusive and practical approach by monitoring signals such as steering angle, lateral acceleration, and lane offset without requiring sensors attached to the driver~\cite{fu2024,fonseca2025}.

Two challenges are potentially critical for vehicle-dynamics-based DDD.
First, \textbf{class imbalance}: drowsy episodes are inherently rare in driving data, with alert states far outnumbering drowsy states, which may bias classifiers toward the majority class and impair detection of safety-critical events.
Second, \textbf{domain shift}: driving behavior varies across individuals. Because training data typically cover only a subset of potential drivers (in-domain), a deployed model must generalize to unseen drivers (out-domain), whose driving patterns may differ substantially. This inter-driver variability may degrade detection performance.

Improving detection performance under these conditions is a practical priority, yet the degree to which each factor actually degrades performance has not been quantified.
Although class imbalance handling~\cite{chawla2002,he2009} and domain adaptation~\cite{pan2010,weiss2016} have been studied extensively in other contexts, their importance in vehicle-dynamics-based DDD remains unclear.
In particular, it is not known whether these issues materially degrade performance in this modality, whether they warrant dedicated countermeasures, and how they interact when both are present.
To this end, we pose the following research questions:
\begin{description}
\item[\textbf{RQ1.}] To what extent do class imbalance and domain shift each affect vehicle-dynamics-based DDD performance, as quantified through the four design factors ($R$, $M$, $D$, $G$)?
\item[\textbf{RQ2.}] Do the contributing factors interact, and if so, what is the optimal factor combination?
\end{description}

This paper addresses these research questions by constructing a DDD framework using a public open dataset and conducting a \textbf{four-factor factorial experiment} to quantify the effects of class imbalance handling and domain configuration on detection performance.
The four factors---rebalancing strategy ($R$), training mode ($M$), distance metric ($D$), and domain membership ($G$)---are systematically varied across 1,512 random forest runs, and Sobol--Hoeffding variance decomposition is applied to identify which factors significantly affect performance and which combinations are practically effective.
Of these, rebalancing strategy ($R$) addresses class imbalance, while training mode ($M$), distance metric ($D$), and domain membership ($G$) address domain configuration.
The main contributions are:

\begin{enumerate}
\item We construct a DDD experimental framework on a publicly available 87-driver dataset that enables joint, controlled analysis of class imbalance handling and domain shift under a unified factorial design.

\item Through a four-factor factorial experiment with Sobol--Hoeffding variance decomposition, we quantify the relative importance of these design factors and reveal that training mode and class rebalancing dominate ($> 95$\% of systematic variance), with a substantial $R \times M$ interaction that reverses the optimal strategy across training modes.

\item We propose subject-wise SMOTE (SW-SMOTE), which applies oversampling independently within each driver's data to preserve individual driving patterns, and show through exhaustive factor-combination search that SW-SMOTE with within-domain training maximizes threshold-independent discrimination (AUROC = 0.903, AUPRC = 0.648; within-domain F2 = 0.558).
\end{enumerate}

Section~\ref{sec:related} reviews related work.

Section~\ref{sec:framework} describes the four-factor experimental design, common settings, and analysis methods.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Vehicle-Dynamics-Based Drowsy Driving Detection}
Vehicle dynamics signals---steering wheel angle, lateral acceleration, and lane position---are widely used for DDD because they enable non-intrusive monitoring.
Arefnezhad et~al.~\cite{arefnezhad2019} used statistical and spectral features with SVM, Baccour et~al.~\cite{baccour2022} compared vehicle-based and driver-based features for drowsiness monitoring using SVM with class weighting, and Sun et~al.~\cite{sun2021} optimized per-driver calculation parameters to account for driver fingerprinting differences.
Zhao et~al.~\cite{zhao2012} applied continuous wavelet transform features to steering signals with SVM, and Wang et~al.~\cite{wang2022} used LSTM on five vehicle signals, although their study targeted driver distraction rather than drowsiness.
More recently, Zhu et~al.~\cite{zhu2025} proposed MADFM, a multi-layer adaptive fatigue monitoring model based on steering wheel signals.
Recent surveys~\cite{fu2024,fonseca2025,alquraishi2024,perkins2022} confirm the practical relevance of this modality.
However, most studies evaluate a single method on a fixed split, and the sensitivity of detection performance to data preprocessing choices has received little attention.

\subsection{Class Imbalance in Drowsy Driving Detection}
Class imbalance is inherent in drowsy driving data because drowsy episodes are rare but safety-critical.
He and Garcia~\cite{he2009} summarize major countermeasures such as random under-sampling (RUS), Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique (SMOTE)~\cite{chawla2002}, and cost-sensitive learning, but none of these standard techniques account for the multi-subject structure of pooled driving data.
In vehicle-dynamics-based DDD, only a few studies explicitly mention imbalance handling: Yadawadkar et~al.~\cite{yadawadkar2018} compared SMOTE with no resampling, Schwarz et~al.~\cite{schwarz2019} used SMOTE in steering-based detection, and Baccour et~al.~\cite{baccour2022} used class weighting when comparing vehicle-dynamics-based and driver-based features.
However, these studies treat imbalance handling as an auxiliary choice rather than the main object of study, and systematic comparison of multiple countermeasures remains lacking.
Moreover, the effectiveness of resampling may depend on the characteristics of the target class, such as temporal clustering and inter-subject variability in drowsy episodes, which require dedicated treatment beyond standard techniques.

\subsection{Domain Shift in Drowsy Driving Detection}
Inter-subject variability creates domain shift in DDD~\cite{pan2010,aravinth2025}.
In vehicle-dynamics-based DDD and related driver-state studies, Sun et~al.~\cite{sun2021} examined subject-independent drowsiness detection while highlighting driver fingerprinting differences, and Maritsch et~al.~\cite{maritsch2022} used leave-one-subject-out evaluation for vehicle-dynamics-based driver-state prediction.
These studies recognize cross-subject variability, but systematic comparison of domain-related design choices in vehicle-dynamics-based DDD remains limited.

\label{sec:formulation}

\subsection{DDD Framework Overview}\label{sec:ddd_overview}
Vehicle-dynamics-based DDD monitors vehicle signals---such as steering angle, lateral acceleration, and lane position---to infer the driver's alertness level without requiring on-body sensors.
Fig.~\ref{fig:framework_general} sketches the generic processing pipeline that is shared, with minor variations, across most prior vehicle-dynamics-based DDD studies: per-subject signal segmentation and labeling, feature extraction, chronological train/validation/test split, classifier training, probability calibration, threshold optimization, and held-out test-set evaluation.

% Notation verified: \mathcal{S}, \mathcal{D}_s, \mathbf{x}_w, y_w \in {0,1}
%   — standard ML dataset notation (He \& Garcia 2009; Chawla et al. 2002)
%   — y=0 (alert), y=1 (drowsy): positive class = minority, consistent with
%     He \& Garcia (2009) convention
Let $\mathcal{S} = \{s_1, \ldots, s_N\}$ denote the set of $N$ subjects.
For each subject~$s$, vehicle signals are segmented into fixed-length time windows, and each window is assigned a binary drowsiness label based on a subjective sleepiness scale, yielding a per-subject dataset $\mathcal{D}_s = \{(\mathbf{x}_w, y_w)\}_{w=1}^{n_s}$, where $\mathbf{x}_w \in \mathbb{R}^p$ is a feature vector extracted from the window and $y_w \in \{0, 1\}$ is the drowsiness label ($0$: alert, $1$: drowsy).

Each subject's dataset is split chronologically into three disjoint portions:
% Notation verified: \dot{\cup} (disjoint union)
%   — standard set-theoretic notation for partition into mutually exclusive subsets
%   — clearer than plain \cup for emphasizing no overlap (data leakage prevention)
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:split}
\mathcal{D}_s = \mathcal{D}_s^{\mathrm{tr}} \,\dot{\cup}\, \mathcal{D}_s^{\mathrm{val}} \,\dot{\cup}\, \mathcal{D}_s^{\mathrm{te}}
\end{equation}
with a 70/15/15 ratio, preserving temporal order to prevent data leakage from future windows.
The \emph{training set} $\mathcal{D}_s^{\mathrm{tr}}$ is used to fit the classifier, the \emph{validation set} $\mathcal{D}_s^{\mathrm{val}}$ for hyperparameter selection and threshold tuning, and the \emph{test set} $\mathcal{D}_s^{\mathrm{te}}$ for final performance evaluation.

% Notation verified: f: R^p -> {0,1}, D_pool = \bigcup D_s^{tr}
%   — classifier mapping notation standard in He \& Garcia (2009)
%   — pooled training set with subset S' \subseteq S deferred to Sec.4 (Eq. train_pool)
The DDD task is to learn a classifier $f\colon \mathbb{R}^p \to \{0, 1\}$ from pooled training data $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{pool}} = \bigcup_{s \in \mathcal{S}'} \mathcal{D}_s^{\mathrm{tr}}$ (where $\mathcal{S}' \subseteq \mathcal{S}$ depends on the training mode) that generalizes to unseen test windows.
The specific dataset, signal set, and preprocessing parameters are detailed in Section~\ref{sec:common_settings}.

\subsection{Class Imbalance}\label{sec:class_imbalance}
% Notation verified: n_c(D), \rho = n_1/n_0, rebalancing operator D -> \tilde{D}
%   — set-cardinality class count: consistent with He \& Garcia (2009), Chawla et al. (2002)
%   — imbalance ratio \rho: standard definition (minority/majority < 1)
%   — rebalancing operator with target ratio r: aligns with SMOTE (Chawla et al. 2002)
%     and RUS formulations in He \& Garcia (2009)
For a given dataset $\mathcal{D}$, let $n_c(\mathcal{D}) = |\{(\mathbf{x}_w, y_w) \in \mathcal{D} : y_w = c\}|$ denote the number of windows with label~$c$, where $c = 0$ (alert) and $c = 1$ (drowsy).
Because drowsy episodes are rare, $n_1 \ll n_0$, yielding an imbalance ratio $\rho = n_1 / n_0 \ll 1$.
A rebalancing operator transforms the training set $\mathcal{D}$ into $\tilde{\mathcal{D}}$ so that the adjusted ratio $\tilde{n}_1 / \tilde{n}_0$ approaches a target value~$r$, either by under-sampling the majority class or over-sampling the minority class.

\subsection{Domain Shift}\label{sec:domain_shift}
% Notation verified: P_s(x), covariate shift P(y|x) shared, G_src \cap G_tgt = \emptyset
%   — per-domain marginal P_s(x): follows Pan \& Yang (2010, Sec. 2) notation
%   — covariate shift (P_s(x) differs, P(y|x) shared): Pan \& Yang (2010, Definition 1)
%   — source/target partition with empty intersection: standard transfer learning setup
%     (Pan \& Yang 2010; Gretton et al. 2012)
Each subject~$s$ induces a marginal feature distribution $P_s(\mathbf{x})$.
Inter-driver variability means that $P_{s_i}(\mathbf{x}) \neq P_{s_j}(\mathbf{x})$ in general; following the transfer-learning taxonomy of Pan and Yang~\cite{pan2010}, this constitutes covariate shift when the labeling mechanism $P(y \mid \mathbf{x})$ is approximately shared across subjects.
When subjects are partitioned into a source group $\mathcal{G}_{\mathrm{src}}$ and a target group $\mathcal{G}_{\mathrm{tgt}}$ with $\mathcal{G}_{\mathrm{src}} \cap \mathcal{G}_{\mathrm{tgt}} = \emptyset$, a classifier trained on $\bigcup_{s \in \mathcal{G}_{\mathrm{src}}} \mathcal{D}_s$ may degrade on $\bigcup_{s \in \mathcal{G}_{\mathrm{tgt}}} \mathcal{D}_s$ because the pooled training distribution differs from the target distribution.

These two issues are treated here not as fixed nuisances but as explicit design factors whose practical importance is quantified through the factorial experiment in Section~\ref{sec:framework}.

\section{Experimental Framework}
\label{sec:framework}

To answer the two research questions posed in Section~I---which factors materially affect performance (RQ1) and whether their interactions determine the optimal design (RQ2)---we design a four-factor factorial experiment.
Sobol--Hoeffding variance decomposition (Section~\ref{sec:analysis_methods}) quantifies each factor's individual and interactive contribution, enabling a principled answer to both questions from a single unified experiment.

Section~\ref{sec:design_overview} provides an overview of the factorial design; Section~\ref{sec:factor_defs} defines each factor; Section~\ref{sec:common_settings} describes common experimental settings; and Section~\ref{sec:analysis_methods} presents the analysis methods.

\subsection{Overview of the Factorial Experiment}\label{sec:design_overview}
The experiment follows a four-factor full-factorial design.
Fig.~\ref{fig:framework_factors} shows the dependency chain of the four study-specific factors---$D$, $G$, $M$, and $R$---each acting on the data partition produced by its predecessor; the resulting $(R, M, D, G)$ cell is then evaluated using the generic DDD pipeline of Fig.~\ref{fig:framework_general}. This chain operationalizes the formulation of Section~\ref{sec:formulation} as follows.

Starting from the subject set $\mathcal{S}$ with per-subject datasets $\mathcal{D}_s = \{(\mathbf{x}_w, y_w)\}$ (Section~\ref{sec:ddd_overview}), a \emph{distance metric}~$D$ first computes pairwise inter-subject distances and partitions $\mathcal{S}$ into an in-domain group $\mathcal{G}_{\mathrm{src}}$ (44~subjects) and an out-domain group $\mathcal{G}_{\mathrm{tgt}}$ (43~subjects), instantiating the domain split of Section~\ref{sec:domain_shift}.
\emph{Domain membership}~$G$ then selects which of these two groups serves as the evaluation target $\mathcal{G}_{\mathrm{eval}}$.
Each subject's data is split chronologically into $(\mathcal{D}_s^{\mathrm{tr}}, \mathcal{D}_s^{\mathrm{val}}, \mathcal{D}_s^{\mathrm{te}})$ per Eq.~\eqref{eq:split} (70/15/15 ratio).
\emph{Training mode}~$M$ assembles the pooled training set $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{pool}} = \bigcup_{s \in \mathcal{S}_M} \mathcal{D}_s^{\mathrm{tr}}$ from a subset $\mathcal{S}_M \subseteq \mathcal{S}$ determined by the partition.
After feature extraction and selection ($p = 145 \to k = 10$), \emph{rebalancing strategy}~$R$ applies the operator of Section~\ref{sec:class_imbalance} to produce $\tilde{\mathcal{D}}$ with adjusted class ratio approaching the target~$r$.
The classifier $f\colon \mathbb{R}^p \to \{0,1\}$ (Section~\ref{sec:ddd_overview}) is trained on $\tilde{\mathcal{D}}$, calibrated, and evaluated on $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{test}} = \bigcup_{s \in \mathcal{G}_{\mathrm{eval}}} \mathcal{D}_s^{\mathrm{te}}$.

The four factors thus form a dependency chain $D \to G \to M \to R$, where each downstream factor operates on the data subset constructed by its predecessors.
Each factorial cell is uniquely determined by a quadruple $(R, M, D, G)$; Section~\ref{sec:factor_defs} defines the levels of each factor (summarized in Table~\ref{tab:factorial}) and the total number of observations.

\subsubsection{Distance Metric ($D$) and Domain Grouping}\label{sec:grouping}
To instantiate the source/target partition $(\mathcal{G}_{\mathrm{src}}, \mathcal{G}_{\mathrm{tgt}})$ defined in Section~\ref{sec:formulation}, pairwise distances $d(s_i, s_j)$ between all 87 subjects are computed from the per-subject window sets $\mathcal{D}_{s_i}$ and $\mathcal{D}_{s_j}$ using three metrics:

\textbf{Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD)}~\cite{gretton2012} measures distributional difference in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space~$\mathcal{H}$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:mmd}
\mathrm{MMD}^2(s_i, s_j) = \left\| \frac{1}{n_{s_i}} \sum_{w \in s_i} \phi(\mathbf{x}_w) - \frac{1}{n_{s_j}} \sum_{w \in s_j} \phi(\mathbf{x}_w) \right\|_{\mathcal{H}}^2
\end{equation}
where $\phi$ is the feature map induced by a Gaussian kernel.

\textbf{Dynamic Time Warping (DTW)}~\cite{berndt1994} aligns two univariate time series and finds the warping path that minimizes cumulative Euclidean distance.
Each subject's multivariate window sequence is first reduced to a scalar series $\bar{x}_t^{(i)} = \frac{1}{p}\sum_{d=1}^{p} x_{t,d}^{(i)}$ (mean across all $z$-scored features at each time step), and DTW is computed as:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:dtw}
\mathrm{DTW}(s_i, s_j) = \sqrt{\min_{\pi \in \mathcal{P}} \sum_{(a,b) \in \pi} \bigl(\bar{x}_a^{(i)} - \bar{x}_b^{(j)}\bigr)^2}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{P}$ is the set of admissible warping paths.
Because DTW with Euclidean cost is symmetric, no explicit symmetrization is required.

\textbf{Wasserstein Distance}~\cite{villani2009} computes the minimum transport cost between the empirical feature distributions:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:wasserstein}
W_1(s_i, s_j) = \inf_{\gamma \in \Gamma(P_{s_i}, P_{s_j})} \int \|\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x}'\|\, d\gamma(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x}')
\end{equation}
where $\Gamma(P_{s_i}, P_{s_j})$ is the set of couplings with marginals $P_{s_i}$ and $P_{s_j}$.

For each metric, a $K$-nearest-neighbor (KNN) score ($K = 5$) is computed per subject as the mean distance to its $K$ nearest neighbors:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:knn_score}
\mathrm{KNN}_K(s) = \frac{1}{K} \sum_{j \in \mathcal{N}_K(s)} d(s, s_j)
\end{equation}
Subjects are split at the median of $\mathrm{KNN}_K$ into \textbf{in-domain} (44 subjects, lowest scores, most typical) and \textbf{out-domain} (43 subjects, highest scores, most dissimilar), forming the concrete $\mathcal{G}_{\mathrm{src}}$ and $\mathcal{G}_{\mathrm{tgt}}$ used in the factorial design.

\textbf{Distance metric} $D \in \{\mathrm{MMD}, \mathrm{DTW}, W_1\}$ determines the pairwise subject distances and the binary domain partition $(\mathcal{G}_{\mathrm{src}}, \mathcal{G}_{\mathrm{tgt}})$ via Eqs.~\eqref{eq:mmd}--\eqref{eq:knn_score}.

\subsubsection{Domain Membership ($G$)}
\textbf{Domain membership} $G \in \{\mathrm{in}, \mathrm{out}\}$ selects the evaluation-target group:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:eval_group}
\mathcal{G}_{\mathrm{eval}} =
\begin{cases}
\mathcal{G}_{\mathrm{src}} & \text{if } G = \mathrm{in}\;\text{(44 subjects)} \\
\mathcal{G}_{\mathrm{tgt}} & \text{if } G = \mathrm{out}\;\text{(43 subjects)}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Training Mode ($M$)}
\textbf{Training mode} $M \in \{\mathrm{Cross}, \mathrm{Within}, \mathrm{Mixed}\}$ assembles the training pool from subjects determined by $\mathcal{G}_{\mathrm{eval}}$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:train_pool}
\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{pool}} = \bigcup_{s \,\in\, \mathcal{S}_M} \mathcal{D}_s^{\mathrm{tr}}, \quad
\mathcal{S}_M =
\begin{cases}
\mathcal{S} \setminus \mathcal{G}_{\mathrm{eval}} & M = \mathrm{Cross} \\
\mathcal{G}_{\mathrm{eval}} & M = \mathrm{Within} \\
\mathcal{S} & M = \mathrm{Mixed}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
Cross-domain trains exclusively on the opposite group, within-domain on the evaluation group itself, and mixed on all 87 subjects.

\subsubsection{Rebalancing Strategy ($R$)}
\textbf{Rebalancing strategy} $R$ applies a rebalancing operator $\mathcal{R}$ to the training pool, yielding $\tilde{\mathcal{D}} = \mathcal{R}(\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{pool}})$ with adjusted class ratio $\tilde{n}_1/\tilde{n}_0$.
Seven levels are defined:
\begin{itemize}
\item Baseline (BL): $\tilde{\mathcal{D}} = \mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{pool}}$ (identity; original ratio $\rho$ preserved).
\item RUS at $r \in \{0.1, 0.5\}$: randomly discard majority-class samples until $\tilde{n}_1 / \tilde{n}_0 = r$.
\item SMOTE~\cite{chawla2002} at $r \in \{0.1, 0.5\}$: generate synthetic minority-class samples via $k$-NN interpolation on $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{pool}}$ until $\tilde{n}_1 / \tilde{n}_0 = r$.
\item SW-SMOTE at $r \in \{0.1, 0.5\}$: apply SMOTE independently within each subject before pooling:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:swsmote}
\tilde{\mathcal{D}} = \bigcup_{s \in \mathcal{S}_M} \mathrm{SMOTE}_r(\mathcal{D}_s^{\mathrm{tr}})
\end{equation}
so that synthetic samples preserve individual driving patterns rather than interpolating across subjects.
\end{itemize}

The classifier $f$ is trained on $\tilde{\mathcal{D}}$ and evaluated on $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{test}} = \bigcup_{s \in \mathcal{G}_{\mathrm{eval}}} \mathcal{D}_s^{\mathrm{te}}$.

\subsection{Common Experimental Settings}\label{sec:common_settings}

\subsubsection{Dataset and Signals}\label{sec:dataset}
The publicly available driving dataset of Aygun et~al.~\cite{aygun2024} is used, comprising $N = 87$ subjects each recorded over two sessions on the SIMlsl driving simulator at $f_s = 60$~Hz.
Five raw signals are extracted (Table~\ref{tab:signals}).

\begin{table}[!t]
\caption{Vehicle Dynamics Signals\label{tab:signals}}
\centering
\begin{tabular}{|c|l|c|}
\hline
Symbol & Signal & Unit \\
\hline
$\delta(t)$ & Steering angle & rad \\
\hline
$\dot{\delta}(t)$ & Steering speed & rad/s \\
\hline
$a_y(t)$ & Lateral acceleration & m/s$^2$ \\
\hline
$a_x(t)$ & Longitudinal acceleration & m/s$^2$ \\

Under the kinematic bicycle model~\cite{rajamani2012}, lateral acceleration follows:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bicycle}
a_y = \frac{v^2}{L}\,\delta
\end{equation}
where $v$ is vehicle speed and $L$ is the wheelbase.
Lane offset evolves as:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:lane_offset}
\ddot{e}_{\text{lane}}(t) = a_y(t) - a_{y,\text{road}}(t)
\end{equation}
Because $\dot{\delta} = d\delta/dt$ and $a_y \propto \delta$, four of the five signals ($\delta$, $\dot{\delta}$, $a_y$, $e_{\text{lane}}$) are deterministically coupled through a single degree of freedom $\delta(t)$, with only $a_x$ dynamically independent.

\subsubsection{Data Preprocessing}\label{sec:preprocessing}
Raw signals are segmented into 3-second sliding windows with 50\% overlap (1.5-second step).
Each window is labeled using the Karolinska Sleepiness Scale (KSS)~\cite{akerstedt1990}: KSS~$\in \{1, \ldots, 5\}$ maps to Alert (class~0) and KSS~$\in \{8, 9\}$ to Drowsy (class~1); intermediate scores (6, 7) are excluded to maximize label reliability.

For each window, three groups of descriptors are extracted from the five vehicle signals ($\delta$, $\dot{\delta}$, $a_y$, $a_x$, $e_{\text{lane}}$): (i) the 18 statistical and spectral descriptors of Arefnezhad et~al.~\cite{arefnezhad2019} per signal, computed on each signal's raw waveform and its FFT power spectrum in the $[0.5, 30]$~Hz band---Range, StdDev, Energy, Zero-Crossing Rate, Quartiles (Q25, Median, Q75), Skewness, Kurtosis, Katz Fractal Dimension, Shannon Entropy, Frequency Variance, Spectral Entropy, Spectral Flux, Frequency Center of Gravity, Dominant Frequency, Average PSD, and Sample Entropy ($5 \times 18 = 90$ features); (ii) per-signal smoothed-trend, standard-deviation, and prediction-error descriptors ($5 \times 3 = 15$ features); and (iii) per-signal permutation entropy over the eight length-3 ordinal patterns ($5 \times 8 = 40$ features).
The full candidate pool is therefore $p = 145$ features per window.
To reduce dimensionality and mitigate overfitting, the candidate features are ranked by random forest feature importance~\cite{breiman2001} (200 trees, balanced class weights) and the top $k = 10$ are retained for each training configuration, balancing discriminative power against overfitting given the per-cell sample sizes.

\subsubsection{Classifier and Hyperparameter Optimization}
The classifier $f\colon \mathbb{R}^p \to \{0,1\}$ introduced in Section~\ref{sec:formulation} is instantiated as a random forest (RF)~\cite{breiman2001} and trained on the rebalanced pool $\tilde{\mathcal{D}}$ defined above.
Its hyperparameter vector $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ is tuned independently per factorial cell via Optuna~\cite{akiba2019} with 100 Tree-structured Parzen Estimator (TPE) trials and 3-fold stratified cross-validation:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:hpo}
\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}} = \arg\max_{\boldsymbol{\theta} \in \Theta}\;
  \mathrm{F2}_{\mathrm{CV}}(\boldsymbol{\theta};\, \tilde{\mathcal{D}})
\end{equation}
where $\Theta$ is the search space defined in Table~\ref{tab:hpo}.
After Optuna selection, the classification threshold $\tau$ is swept over the set of unique predicted probabilities derived from the precision--recall curve on the validation set, retaining the value that maximizes F2:
\begin{multline}
\label{eq:threshold}
\hat{\tau} = \arg\max_{\tau \in [0,1]}\;
  \mathrm{F2}\!\Bigl(\bigl\{ (\hat{p}_w,\, y_w) :\\
  (\mathbf{x}_w, y_w) \in \textstyle\bigcup_{s \in \mathcal{G}_{\mathrm{eval}}} \mathcal{D}_s^{\mathrm{val}} \bigr\},\; \tau\Bigr)
\end{multline}
where $\hat{p}_w = \Pr(y{=}1 \mid \mathbf{x}_w;\, \hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}})$ is the RF posterior probability.
The final model is calibrated via Platt scaling~\cite{platt1999} (5-fold) on the combined train + validation data, yielding calibrated probabilities $\hat{p}_w^{\mathrm{cal}}$.
During HPO, Optuna selects \texttt{class\_weight} $\in$ \{balanced, balanced\_subsample\} to guide tree construction.
For the final evaluation, a fixed class weighting of $\{0{:}1.0,\; 1{:}3.0\}$ is applied to all configurations to ensure a uniform minority-recall emphasis across factorial cells, decoupling the class-weight choice from the rebalancing factor $R$.
Each observation in the factorial experiment is therefore:
\begin{align}
\label{eq:observation}
Y_{R,M,D,G} &= \mathrm{Metric}\!\bigl(\bigl\{ (\hat{y}_w,\, y_w) : (\mathbf{x}_w, y_w) \in \mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{test}} \bigr\}\bigr), \nonumber\\
\hat{y}_w &= \mathbb{1}\!\left[\hat{p}_w^{\mathrm{cal}} \geq \hat{\tau}\right]
\end{align}
where $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{test}} = \bigcup_{s \in \mathcal{G}_{\mathrm{eval}}} \mathcal{D}_s^{\mathrm{te}}$ and $\mathrm{Metric} \in \{\mathrm{F2}, \mathrm{AUROC}, \mathrm{AUPRC}\}$.

\textbf{Computational cost.}
Each of the 1,512 training runs (126 cells $\times$ 12 seeds) involves 100 Optuna trials with 3-fold CV, totalling $\approx 4.5 \times 10^5$ model fits.
Individual HPC jobs required 4 CPU cores, 8--10~GB RAM, and 6--8 hours wall time; the entire experiment consumed $\approx 10{,}000$ core-hours on the university cluster.

\textbf{Inference latency.}
A trained RF with $\leq 1{,}500$ trees and $k = 10$ input features produces a prediction in $< 1$~ms on a single CPU core, well within the 3-second window update interval.

\subsubsection{Evaluation Metrics}\label{sec:metrics}
The observation $Y_{R,M,D,G}$ in Eq.~\eqref{eq:observation} is computed with three complementary metrics chosen to capture different aspects of imbalanced classification:

\textbf{F2-score} is the threshold-dependent, recall-weighted harmonic mean of precision ($P$) and recall ($R$):
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:f2}
\mathrm{F2} = \frac{5\,P\,R}{4\,P + R}
\end{equation}
The $\beta = 2$ weighting penalizes missed drowsy episodes more heavily than false alarms, reflecting the safety-critical nature of DDD.
F2 is used as the primary optimization target in Eqs.~\eqref{eq:hpo} and~\eqref{eq:threshold}.

\textbf{AUROC} (area under the ROC curve) summarizes threshold-free discrimination ability over the full operating range.
It complements F2 by assessing whether the model ranks drowsy windows above alert ones regardless of threshold choice.

\textbf{AUPRC} (area under the precision--recall curve)~\cite{saito2015} provides a threshold-free measure that is more informative than AUROC under severe class imbalance, because it does not credit correct majority-class predictions.

Together, these three metrics form a minimal sufficient set: one threshold-dependent recall-focused metric (F2), one threshold-free global metric (AUROC), and one threshold-free imbalance-aware metric (AUPRC).

\subsection{Analysis Methods}\label{sec:analysis_methods}
Three analysis methods are applied to the 1,512 evaluation results: an intuitive OFAT scan, a Sobol--Hoeffding variance decomposition, and a statistical framework for effect sizes and null-claim support.

\subsubsection{One-Factor-at-a-Time (OFAT) Analysis}
Before the quantitative decomposition, we perform an OFAT analysis to build intuition.
For each target factor, all other factors are fixed at every possible combination, and the mean performance is plotted across the target factor's levels.
Each resulting line represents one fixed condition (averaged over 12 seeds), producing a ``spaghetti plot'' that reveals: (i)~whether the factor's effect is consistent across conditions, (ii)~how much the estimate varies depending on which conditions are fixed, and (iii)~the magnitude of the factor's effect relative to the spread across conditions.

\subsubsection{Variance-Based Sensitivity Analysis (Sobol Indices)}
To quantify the relative importance of each factor and their interactions, we adopt the Sobol--Hoeffding functional ANOVA decomposition~\cite{sobol1993,saltelli2008}.
Let the response surface $Y = g(X_1, \ldots, X_k)$ map factor configurations to performance, where $Y \equiv Y_{R,M,D,G}$ (Eq.~\eqref{eq:observation}) and $X_i \in \{R, M, D, G\}$.
The total variance admits a unique orthogonal decomposition:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:sobol_decomp}
V(Y) = \sum_{i=1}^{k} V_i + \sum_{i<j} V_{ij} + \cdots + V_{1,2,\ldots,k}
\end{equation}
where $V_i = \mathrm{Var}_{X_i}\bigl[\mathbb{E}_{X_{\sim i}}(Y \mid X_i)\bigr]$ is the variance of the conditional expectation over factor~$i$ alone, and $V_{ij}$ captures the joint effect of $(X_i, X_j)$ not explained by their individual contributions.
The first-order Sobol index normalises each contribution:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:sobol_first}
S_i = \frac{V_i}{V(Y)}
\end{equation}
and the total-order index aggregates the main effect with all interactions involving factor~$i$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:sobol_total}
S_{Ti} = 1 - \frac{V_{\sim i}}{V(Y)}
\end{equation}
where $V_{\sim i} = \mathrm{Var}_{X_{\sim i}}\bigl[\mathbb{E}_{X_i}(Y \mid X_{\sim i})\bigr]$ is the variance explained by all factors \emph{except}~$i$.
The difference $S_{Ti} - S_i$ quantifies the fraction of variance attributable to interactions involving factor~$i$.
Because our design is balanced, all sum-of-squares terms are computed exactly via marginal means without Monte Carlo sampling.
Confidence intervals (95\%) are obtained by percentile bootstrap ($B = 2{,}000$) resampling over the 12 seeds.

\subsubsection{Statistical Framework}
Effect sizes between factor levels are measured by Cliff's $\delta$~\cite{cliff1993} ($< 0.147$ negligible, $< 0.33$ small, $< 0.474$ medium, $\geq 0.474$ large).
Claims of factor irrelevance (e.g., distance metric equivalence) are supplemented with Bayes factors via BIC approximation~\cite{wagenmakers2007}, interpreted on the Jeffreys scale~\cite{jeffreys1961}.

\textbf{Global validation.}
A permutation test ($B = 10{,}000$) validates the overall rebalancing effect.
Seed convergence analysis confirms ranking stability across the 12 random seeds.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Answer to RQ1: Relative Contribution of Each Factor}\label{sec:rq1}
The Sobol decomposition reveals a concentrated variance structure: $M$, $R$, and $R \times M$ jointly explain $> 95$\% of systematic variance (Eq.~\ref{eq:systematic}), while $D$ and $G$ together contribute $< 5$\%.

To understand why training mode dominates, define the \emph{subject overlap ratio} between the training pool $\mathcal{S}_M$ (Eq.~\eqref{eq:train_pool}) and the evaluation group $\mathcal{G}_{\mathrm{eval}}$ (Eq.~\eqref{eq:eval_group}):
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:overlap}
\omega_M = \frac{|\mathcal{S}_M \cap \mathcal{G}_{\mathrm{eval}}|}{|\mathcal{G}_{\mathrm{eval}}|}
\end{equation}
By construction, $\omega_{\mathrm{Cross}} = 0$ (training excludes evaluation subjects), while $\omega_{\mathrm{Within}} = \omega_{\mathrm{Mixed}} = 1$ (evaluation subjects are included).
This binary switch between $\omega = 0$ and $\omega = 1$ determines whether target-subject patterns are available during training, which in turn governs the performance bottleneck and the effectiveness of rebalancing (Section~\ref{sec:rq2}).

In contrast, distance metric and domain membership are negligible ($S_{TD} < 0.015$, $S_{TG} < 0.031$; $BF_{01} = 71$--$767$).
Three fundamentally different metrics---MMD (distributional), DTW (temporal), and Wasserstein (geometric)---produce equivalent downstream performance despite generating different subject rankings (Table~\ref{tab:spearman}; DTW vs.\ MMD: $\rho = 0.12$, $p = 0.29$).

\subsection{Answer to RQ2: Interaction and Optimal Factor Combination}\label{sec:rq2}
The $R \times M$ interaction ($S_{R \times M} = 0.212$ for F2, concentrated at 87--96\% of each dominant factor's interaction variance) confirms that the contributing factors do not act independently.
This interaction manifests as a mode-dependent strategy reversal: the \emph{identity} of the best rebalancing strategy changes across training modes (Table~\ref{tab:permode}; Fig.~\ref{fig:ranking_reversal}).
A practitioner who selects a rebalancing strategy based on cross-domain results would deploy the \emph{worst} strategy for within-domain operation, and vice versa.

The mechanistic origin is a mode-dependent performance bottleneck.
The mode-dependent rebalancing gain, averaged over $D$ and $G$, is:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:rebalancing_gain}
\Delta Y_R^{(M)} = \mathbb{E}_{D,G}\!\left[Y_{R,M,D,G} - Y_{\mathrm{BL},M,D,G}\right]
\end{equation}
For the best strategy (SW-SMOTE $r{=}0.1$), the empirical values are:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bottleneck}
\Delta Y_{\text{SW-SM.}}^{(M)} =
\begin{cases}
+0.343\;(+159\%) & M = \mathrm{Within}\;(\omega = 1) \\
+0.319\;(+119\%) & M = \mathrm{Mixed}\;\;(\omega = 1) \\
-0.059\;(-37\%)   & M = \mathrm{Cross}\;\;\;(\omega = 0)
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
When $\omega_M = 1$, the performance bottleneck is class imbalance ($\rho \ll 1$), and oversampling is effective.
When $\omega_M = 0$, the bottleneck shifts to distributional mismatch ($P_{\mathrm{pool}}(\mathbf{x}) \neq P_{\mathrm{test}}(\mathbf{x})$), and all strategies collapse to near-chance levels (F2 $= 0.04$--$0.17$).
This sign reversal of $\Delta Y_R^{(M)}$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:bottleneck}) is the mechanistic origin of the $R \times M$ interaction.
The $G \times M$ interaction is negligible ($S_{G \times M} = 0.006$--$0.009$), an order of magnitude below $S_{R \times M}$.
The optimal factor combination is therefore SW-SMOTE $r{=}0.1$ with $\omega_M = 1$; within-domain training maximizes threshold-independent discrimination (AUROC = 0.903, AUPRC = 0.648; within-domain F2 = 0.558), while Mixed mode trades 0.024 AUROC for a higher recall-leaning F2 (0.587).

\subsection{Practical Implications}
The Sobol total-order indices establish a strict factor priority ordering for practitioners deploying vehicle-dynamics-based DDD systems:
\begin{multline}
\label{eq:priority}
S_{TM}\,(0.48\text{--}0.66) \;>\; S_{TR}\,(0.40\text{--}0.46) \\
\gg\; S_{TG}\,({<}0.031) \;\approx\; S_{TD}\,({<}0.015)
\end{multline}
This ordering, combined with the subject overlap ratio $\omega_M$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:overlap}), yields the following decision rules:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Training mode (highest priority):} Use within-domain or mixed training whenever target-subject data is available ($\omega_M = 1$).

\item \textbf{Class rebalancing (second priority):} The optimal rebalancing strategy is mode-dependent (Eq.~\ref{eq:bottleneck}).
When $\omega_M = 1$, apply SW-SMOTE at $r = 0.1$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:recommended}
R^{*}(\omega_M)\!=\!
\begin{cases}
\text{SW-SMOTE},\;r{=}0.1 & \!\omega_M{=}1 \\
\text{any (none helps)} & \!\omega_M{=}0
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta Y = +159\%$ (F2) for $\omega_M = 1$ and $\Delta Y \leq 0$ for $\omega_M = 0$.

\item \textbf{Distance metric and domain membership (negligible):} Use any convenient metric for domain grouping.
The choice has no measurable impact on classification performance ($BF_{01} > 70$).

\item \textbf{Bridging the cross-domain gap ($\omega_M = 0$).}
Since no tested rebalancing strategy improves cross-domain performance (Eq.~\ref{eq:bottleneck}), closing this gap requires reducing the distributional mismatch $P_{\mathrm{pool}}(\mathbf{x}) \neq P_{\mathrm{test}}(\mathbf{x})$ directly.
Promising directions include unsupervised domain adaptation~\cite{pan2010,weiss2016}, few-shot personalization~\cite{kim2025}, and continual learning.
Each can be introduced as an additional factor level within the same Sobol decomposition pipeline, yielding directly comparable variance shares.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Comparison with Prior Work}\label{sec:comparison}
Our finding that class imbalance handling is the dominant design factor aligns with Taylor et~al.~\cite{taylor2021}, who showed that data-related hyperparameters dominate architectural choices in deep learning.
However, our study is the first to demonstrate this in the DDD context and to reveal the interaction with training mode that causes mode-dependent strategy ranking change.
Prior DDD studies that compare rebalancing methods under a single training mode~\cite{he2024,yadawadkar2018} risk selecting a suboptimal strategy for deployment.

The best within-domain performance (F2\,=\,0.558, AUROC\,=\,0.903) is competitive with recent vehicle-dynamics-based methods.
Table~\ref{tab:comparison} positions this work relative to prior DDD studies.
Direct performance comparison is limited because prior studies use different datasets, modalities, and evaluation protocols; the primary contribution here is the factorial decomposition framework, not absolute classification performance.

\subsection{Limitations}\label{sec:limitations}

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Single classifier family.}
The factorial experiment uses RF~\cite{breiman2001}.
The Sobol--Hoeffding framework is classifier-agnostic and can be extended to other learners (e.g., LSTM, SVM) without methodological modification.

\item \textbf{Deterministic data split.}
The train/test partition is deterministic; random seeds vary only model initialisation and resampling.

\item \textbf{Simulator data.}
All data come from a laboratory driving simulator.
On-road conditions introduce additional variability that may alter the absolute variance shares; validation on naturalistic datasets (e.g., SHRP~2) is needed.

\item \textbf{Sample size per cell.}
With $n = 12$, only large effects ($|\delta| > 0.53$) are detectable after Bonferroni correction.

\item \textbf{Feature selection scope.}
Only the top $k = 10$ features from $p = 145$ candidates are used; sensitivity to $k$ and to the full feature set remains to be tested.

\item \textbf{Binary labeling threshold.}
KSS $\leq 5$ vs.\ KSS $\geq 8$ excludes intermediate drowsiness (KSS 6--7), which may limit applicability to graded drowsiness detection scenarios.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
This study quantifies the relative importance of four design factors in vehicle-dynamics-based DDD through a factorial experiment with Sobol--Hoeffding variance decomposition over 1,512 random forest runs from 87 drivers.
Training mode ($S_{TM} = 0.48$--$0.66$) and rebalancing ($S_{TR} = 0.40$--$0.46$), including their interaction ($S_{R \times M} = 0.21$), account for $> 95$\% of systematic variance, whereas distance metric ($S_{TD} < 0.015$; $BF_{01} = 71$--$767$) and domain membership ($S_{TG} < 0.031$) are negligible.
The $R \times M$ interaction reverses the optimal strategy across modes ($\rho_{\text{cross,within}} = -0.74$ to $-0.89$): when evaluation subjects overlap with training data ($\omega_M = 1$), class imbalance is the bottleneck and oversampling is effective; when they do not ($\omega_M = 0$), distributional mismatch dominates and no tested rebalancing strategy helps.
SW-SMOTE $r{=}0.1$ with within-domain training ($\omega_M = 1$) achieves the highest threshold-independent performance (AUROC = 0.903, AUPRC = 0.648; within-domain F2 = 0.558).
Practitioners should prioritize training mode and class rebalancing (Eq.~\ref{eq:priority}); domain configuration requires no optimization.

Future work includes validation with other classifiers (LSTM, SVM), on naturalistic driving data (SHRP~2), and incorporation of domain adaptation and few-shot methods as additional factor levels.
